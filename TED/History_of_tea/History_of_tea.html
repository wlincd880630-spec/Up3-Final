Overview

According to legend, tea was discovered when Shennong, a divine farmer, was poisoned and revived by a leaf. While tea doesn't cure poisonings, its importance to ancient China is undeniable. Archaeological evidence suggests tea cultivation began as early as 6000 years ago.

Originally eaten as a vegetable or cooked with grain porridge, tea transitioned to a drink 1500 years ago. Heating and moisture unlocked its complex flavors, leading to the popular beverage mocha or matcha. This spurred a distinct Chinese tea culture with books, poetry, and even tea art.

Tea spread to Japan in the 9th century and eventually to Europe via Dutch traders in the 1600s. It became highly valued, leading to the creation of Clipper Ships for faster transport. Britain initially paid for tea with silver, then opium, causing the Opium Wars and weakening China.

The British East India Company sought to control tea production by smuggling tea trees and workers from China to India. Today, tea is the second most consumed beverage globally, prepared in diverse ways across cultures.
